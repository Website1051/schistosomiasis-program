Causes of Schistosomiasis

Introduction

Schistosomiasis is caused by parasitic worms known as *Schistosoma*. These parasites thrive in freshwater environments and infect humans through direct skin contact.

The Lifecycle of *Schistosoma*

The transmission of schistosomiasis involves a complex lifecycle:

  • Infected individuals release parasite eggs through urine or feces.
  • Eggs hatch in freshwater, releasing larvae that infect freshwater snails.
  • Inside the snails, the parasites mature and release infectious larvae.
  • These larvae penetrate the skin of people who come into contact with contaminated water.

Environmental Factors

Schistosomiasis thrives in areas with poor sanitation and limited access to clean water. The following environmental conditions contribute to its spread:

  • **Stagnant or slow-moving freshwater** (lakes, rivers, irrigation channels).
  • **Presence of infected freshwater snails**, which act as intermediate hosts.
  • **Lack of proper sanitation**, leading to human waste contaminating water sources.

Human Activities That Contribute to Infection

Certain activities increase the risk of exposure to schistosomiasis, including:

  • Bathing, swimming, or washing clothes in contaminated water.
  • Farming and fishing in infected water bodies.
  • Drinking or using untreated water from lakes and rivers.

Prevention Strategies

To reduce the risk of infection, communities should:

  • Improve access to **clean drinking water and proper sanitation**.
  • Encourage **safe water practices**, such as avoiding direct contact with freshwater in endemic areas.
  • Promote **mass drug administration (MDA)** to treat infected populations.
  • Control snail populations through environmental modifications.
